# Microcosm — Speaker Script

**Audience:** non-technical (executives, LPs, operators, guests)
**Length:** ~5½ minutes spoken (~900 words), plus screen time
**Setup:** have two tabs open before you start — the landing page (`index.html`) and the demo (`demo.html`). On the demo, you'll use the **SPEED 4×** and **SKIP TO END** buttons to control pacing.

Stage directions are in *[brackets and italics]*. Everything else is meant to be spoken, but say it your way — the beats matter more than the exact words.

## 1 · The hook — before you touch the screen (0:00–0:40)

Let me start with a question.

If you were about to spend eighty million dollars on a piece of land — how would you find out whether it's a good idea?

Here's the uncomfortable answer: mostly, you'd guess. You'd hire a consultant, wait six weeks, and pay fifty thousand dollars for a report. You'd commission a survey — where people famously tell you what they *think* they should say, not what they'll actually do. And then a handful of smart people would sit in a conference room and make the call on judgment and gut feel.

Real estate is the biggest asset class in the world, and its biggest decisions are made almost completely blind. You buy the land once. You design the building once. You get one shot — and you find out if you were right *years* later, when it's unfixable.

Every other high-stakes industry gets to rehearse. Boeing flies a plane in a wind tunnel before it flies passengers. Real estate has never had a wind tunnel.

That's what we built.

## 2 · The idea — landing page on screen (0:40–1:30)

This is Microcosm.

In plain terms: we create thousands of AI characters — buyers, renters, neighbors, lenders, investors — each one built to think and behave like a *real* person from a *real* place. Not clones. A retired teacher on a fixed income. A first-time buyer stretching for a mortgage. A neighbor who's worried about traffic.

Then we introduce your decision into their world — a floor plan, a price point, a whole development — and we let them do what people actually do: react, argue, push back, and change each other's minds.

And when the dust settles, you don't get a dashboard full of charts. You get the report: what they'd pay, what they'd fight, what they'd fight *about*, and what to change before you've spent a dollar of real money.

Think of it as a focus group with a thousand people in it — that runs in an afternoon, never gets tired, and includes the exact people you can never get into the room: the skeptical lender, the angry neighbor, the city council.

## 3 · Why this doesn't exist yet (1:30–2:20)

Now — fair question: why doesn't this already exist?

Three reasons, and they all just changed.

First, the science. For fifty years, researchers could simulate crowds, but the simulated people were simple — they followed a few hand-written rules. In the last three years, AI crossed a line: Stanford researchers built simulated people who were compared, head-to-head, against the real humans they were modeled on — and they matched those people's actual survey answers about as consistently as the humans matched *themselves* two weeks later. That was the unlock.

Second, the cost. Running a thousand thinking agents used to be a research-grant expense. Today it costs dollars. The price of this kind of intelligence has been falling by more than ten-x per year.

Third — and this is the gap we're driving through — the companies built on this science so far are *generalists*. They simulate audiences for ads, for politics, for consumer brands. Nobody has built this for the built world: nobody speaks real estate, and nobody checks their simulations against what actually happened — real sales, real lease-ups, real zoning hearings. That checking-against-reality step is our whole company. It's what turns a clever demo into something you can underwrite with.

We don't average away the uncomfortable voices, because in real estate the uncomfortable voice is usually the expensive one.

## 5 · The value, and the close (4:40–5:30)

So what does this mean in dollars?

Today, answering *one* question like this costs fifteen to fifty thousand dollars and takes six weeks. We do it same-day — and the second question, and the tenth, are nearly free. For a homebuilder deciding which floor plans to build, for a developer testing rents and amenities, for anyone walking into a contested zoning hearing — this turns the most expensive guesses in the industry into rehearsals.

And the honest disclaimer, because it builds trust: this is a wind tunnel, not a crystal ball. Every output is labeled as simulation, we publish where it's accurate and where it isn't, and it informs the decision-maker — it never replaces them.

Here's the line I'd leave you with: for a hundred years, real estate has been an industry where you find out if you were right *after* the concrete dries. We think the next great real estate companies will be the ones that got to practice first.

Microcosm is where they'll practice. Thank you.

## Appendix — likely questions, thirty-second answers

**"How do you know it's accurate?"**
Two ways. The science: published studies show these simulated people match real survey responses at roughly 70–95% accuracy, and the landmark Stanford study matched real individuals about as well as those individuals matched themselves over time. Our discipline: we back-test against things that already happened — real sales paces, real lease-ups, real hearing outcomes — and publish the error bars. If we can't beat the traditional methods on past events, we don't deserve the future ones.

**"Is this replacing market research firms?"**
It replaces the *unit of work* — the six-week, five-figure study. The firms themselves are potential partners: some of the best distribution for this is white-labeling it to the advisors the industry already trusts.

**"Couldn't a big AI company just do this?"**
The AI agents, honestly, yes — they're becoming a commodity. What's hard to copy is what we calibrate them *with*: proprietary outcome data from real projects, contributed by the operators who benefit most. The data is the moat, and it compounds.

**"What about fair housing?"**
Bright line: we simulate *markets and decisions* — never individual tenant or buyer screening. Every use case near a protected decision is gated by review, and outputs are always disclosed as synthetic.

**"What does it cost?"**
A team subscription costs less per year than a single consultant study costs today. Enterprise pricing covers the calibrated platform and its integration into underwriting. The pilot pays for itself the first time it changes one decision.
