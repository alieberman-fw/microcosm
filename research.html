Where Microcosm is different

The generative-agent layer is commoditizing. What compounds is vertical: a real estate ontology — product types, submarkets, entitlement, absorption — baked into personas and reports; calibration against actual sales and lease-up outcomes rather than generic survey data; and embedding in underwriting workflows instead of standing beside them. The moat is the outcome data, not the agents.

Founder's memo · 01 — The position

The feasibility study becomes software. We own its calibration.

Within five years, no eight-figure decision in the built world gets made without being simulated first. The $15–50K, six-week feasibility study — and the fielded survey behind it — collapses into a run: a thousand persona-grounded buyers, renters, neighbors, and lenders reacting to the decision in an afternoon, calibrated against what actually happened in that submarket. Microcosm is that layer. Panels first — which floor plan, what mix, what price. Then the metro twin — what absorption, rents, and consent do when the project, or the policy, actually lands. The agents are commoditizing; the calibration corpus is not. And the only place that corpus can be assembled by invitation is a strategic-LP base that owns and operates across every product type. That is the position.
